Babbage's Difference Engine

Background and Motivation

In the early 19th century, mathematical tables were essential tools for navigation, astronomy, engineering, and finance. These tables — listing logarithms, trigonometric values, interest rates, and the like — were computed by hand by teams of human "computers" working in parallel. They were riddled with errors, and those errors had real consequences: ships ran aground, financial calculations went wrong, artillery trajectories missed.

Charles Babbage (1791–1871) became obsessed with this problem. Around 1820, he famously exclaimed to his colleague John Herschel, while checking a set of error-filled tables: "I wish to God these calculations had been executed by steam." This moment is often cited as the seed of his life's work.

What Is the Method of Differences?

The Difference Engine was designed around a mathematical technique called the method of finite differences. Many common mathematical functions — polynomials, logarithms, trigonometric functions — can be approximated by polynomials. And polynomials have a useful property: if you repeatedly take the differences between consecutive values, the differences eventually become constant.

For example, consider the squares: 1, 4, 9, 16, 25...

  • First differences: 3, 5, 7, 9... (each term minus the previous)
  • Second differences: 2, 2, 2, 2... (constant!)

Once you reach a constant difference, you can extend the table mechanically by simple addition alone — no multiplication or division required. This made the method ideally suited to an automatic machine with interlocking gears.

The Machine

Babbage began designing Difference Engine No. 1 in 1822 and received government funding to build it. The design called for around 25,000 precision parts. It would have stood 2.4 meters tall and weighed several tonnes.

It was never completed. Over the course of a decade, Babbage's relationship with his chief engineer Joseph Clement broke down, costs spiraled, and the government eventually withdrew funding in 1842. Only a small demonstration piece was ever assembled in Babbage's lifetime.

Undeterred, Babbage designed a more refined Difference Engine No. 2 in the 1840s — more elegant and requiring fewer parts — but it was also never built in his lifetime.

The Completed Machine

In 1991, to mark the 200th anniversary of Babbage's birth, the Science Museum in London completed Difference Engine No. 2 from Babbage's original drawings. It worked perfectly on the first try, vindicating Babbage's designs and demonstrating that the engineering of his era was, in principle, up to the task. A second complete version was built and is on display at the Computer History Museum in Mountain View, California.

Significance

The Difference Engine was not a general-purpose computer — it could only evaluate polynomials using the method of differences. But it was a landmark in the history of computing: a serious, funded, engineering attempt to automate mathematical calculation. Its failure to be completed, and Babbage's frustration with that failure, directly motivated his far more ambitious next project: the Analytical Engine.
