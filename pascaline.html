Pascal's Computer: The Pascaline

Who Was Blaise Pascal?

Blaise Pascal (1623–1662) was a French mathematician, physicist, and philosopher. He was one of the most brilliant minds of the 17th century. He made foundational contributions to probability theory, projective geometry, fluid mechanics, and philosophy. He was also, at age 18, a practical engineer facing a very concrete problem.

The Problem Pascal Was Solving

Pascal's father, Étienne Pascal, was a tax commissioner in Rouen, France. His job required endless tedious arithmetic, adding up long columns of figures in the old French currency system, which was not decimal. (There were 12 deniers to a sou, and 20 sous to a livre.) Watching his father labor over these calculations, Pascal set out to build a machine that could do the arithmetic automatically.

The Machine

Between 1642 and 1644, Pascal designed and built the Pascaline, a mechanical calculator capable of performing addition and subtraction. The machine consisted of a row of interlocking gears, each representing a digit. Turning a wheel by a certain amount added that value to the running total displayed in windows at the top of the box.

The most clever feature was its automatic carry mechanism: when a gear completed a full rotation (rolling over from 9 to 0), it automatically advanced the next gear by one, just as we carry a 1 when adding digits by hand. This was mechanically tricky to implement reliably, and Pascal went through approximately 50 prototypes before arriving at a working design.

The Pascaline could handle numbers up to 9,999,999. Subtraction was performed using a technique based on nine's complements. This is a workaround required because the carry mechanism only worked in one direction.

Reception and Legacy

Pascal built around 20 machines in total. They were expensive to produce and finicky to operate, and they never became commercially successful. Human computers remained cheaper and more flexible. However, the Pascaline was a genuine technological achievement, the first mechanical calculator known to have actually worked and been used.

Several Pascalines survive today and are held in museums in France and elsewhere.

Pascal's work directly inspired later inventors. Gottfried Wilhelm Leibniz studied the Pascaline and built his own more capable machine, the Stepped Reckoner, in 1694, which could also multiply and divide. The lineage of mechanical calculation runs from Pascal through Leibniz and eventually to Babbage and beyond.

Place in History

The Pascaline predates Babbage's engines by nearly two centuries. Where Babbage's machines were designed to automate entire computations, running through a sequence of operations automatically, the Pascaline simply mechanized a single arithmetic operation at a time, with a human directing each step. The distinction is important: Pascal built a calculator; Babbage aspired to build a computer.

Still, Pascal's insight that arithmetic could be offloaded to a machine was the foundational idea. Everything that followed built on it.
