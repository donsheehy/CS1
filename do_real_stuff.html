Do Real Stuff

There is a trend in higher education when it comes to teaching computer science. It goes like this: no one wants to deal with all the problems that arise from different students working with their own computers. The result is more and more systems designed to hide or simulate the local computer environment.

The advantages seem obvious. You get to focus on the technical learning objectives.

The disadvantages are even more obvious. You don't know how to use your own computer.

Getting into trouble with your environment, your path setup, your Python installation, your git credentials, your virtual environment, or whatever is a good thing. Growth is a function of the number of problems you solve, not the number of problems you avoid.

A Basic Learning Objective: Be Able to Do Stuff

If you finish a course in Python, you should be able ot sit down at your computer and write a working Python program. You shouldn't need an internet connection or a hosted environment.

There is something even worse about this approach. It teaches the students that this computing stuff is too hard for you.

More effort leads to worse results.

The weirdest phenomenon in higher education is that increasing teacher effort leads to worse results. People are surprised to hear this, but it has a simple explanation. When attempting to improve a class, the most natural place to look is at the parts of the course that cause the most problems. What is the most confusing or difficult part. Then, we try to "improve" it.
We add more scaffolding.
We make it easier.
The result: We eliminate the challenges that create the learning opportunities.

Simulated Learning is not Learning

There is a concept of simulation that is fundamental to many aspects of computer science. The idea is that something can simulate another by mimicking the behavior. If the mimic is perfect, then it can be treated as the original.

Take this into the realm of teaching. We decide that those who learn some material will be able to answer certain types of questions. Then, we train students to answer those questions. The goal is not learning, it's just simulated learning.

Some will ask, "Isn't that just as good?" No. The logic only goes one way. If you understand, then you can get the answer. If you can get the answer, you don't necessarily understand.
